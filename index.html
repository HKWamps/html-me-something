    Early Technology Interests

    Mike has always been interested in technology, and first started learning technology while in High School in the early 80's. Learning on a couple of Apple II's that were upgraded with floppy drives over the "tape machine backup" that came with them. His major project was to port over the Basic user's guide for the TRS-80 to the Apple II so the computer class could have a textbook. It was actually used for several years afterwards in the classroom.

    Upon reaching college, the computer program was in the math department, so Mike went the engineering route instead. He still used computers, and the Academy transitioned from the Dartmouth Main Frame to Macintosh's during his tenure at the academy. While in the Coast Guard, he became a systems administrator for the unit's Burroughs Technology Operating System (BTOS) computers and obtained his first personal PC with GW-Basic.


    Actual Technology Career

    Mike earned his MBA from the University of Dayton, and focused on Managment Information Systems and Computer Science. He did such a good job while serving as a graduate assistant, he was hired initially as a developer, but was immediately fleeted up to the technology director position upon the resignation of the previous director. Included in his priorities were all workstation and network management (workstation imaging, network backup, Novell network management, user ID creation and management, etc). He even got to install and set up a donated Tera Data server for the MIS department. Overall there were about 100 staff accounts, 2000 student accounts and two computer labs to maintain. This worked fine until his spouse decided Dayton, Ohio was a drag, and they needed to move to Hong Kong.


    Leveraging Technology in USCG Career

    After returning from Hong Kong, and learning that large white guys who can't speak chinese weren't needed after the handover, Mike returned to Active Duty in the Coast Guard on September 4, 2011. Yes, a week before 9/11. In the 17 years since Mike has been very busy managing the reserve program, And, in every job he has had, he has found numerous uses for this technology skills as the USCG is not the most intricate analysts of data. He leveraged his MS Access and Excel technologies to ascertain information from the largely untapped data sources within the Coast Guard. But, since his primary duties were not in data management, eventually the Coast Guard developed in house expertise who properly locked down the systems and made it harder to obtain information to get the job done. Eventually, promotions took Mike even further from his beloved data, until he was at the end of his career and ready to leave the Coast Guard and "transition" to a new field.

    Future Goals

    Ready to Transition, Mike is eager to return to his passion: developing new ideas, building new systems and squeezing better information from the data that makes up our day to day work life. We'll see where this journey leads . . . .
